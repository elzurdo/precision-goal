\section{Credible Interval Variants: ETI and HDI}\label{app:hdi}

Given a posterior density $p(\theta \mid \mathbf{x})$, a $(1-\alpha)$ credible interval
$[L, U]$ satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:credible_interval}
  \int_L^U p(\theta \mid \mathbf{x})\,d\theta = 1 - \alpha.
\end{equation}
Infinitely many intervals satisfy this constraint; the two most widely used selections are:

\subsection*{Equal-Tailed Interval (ETI)}

The ETI places $\alpha/2$ probability mass in each tail:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eti}
  L_{\rm ETI} = F^{-1}\!\left(\tfrac{\alpha}{2}\right), \qquad
  U_{\rm ETI} = F^{-1}\!\left(1 - \tfrac{\alpha}{2}\right),
\end{equation}
where $F^{-1}$ is the posterior quantile function. The ETI is simple to compute and
uniquely defined, but it is not generally the shortest interval.

\subsection*{Highest Density Interval (HDI)}

The HDI is the shortest interval satisfying Equation~\ref{eq:credible_interval}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hdi}
  [L^*, U^*] = \underset{[L,U]:\;\int_L^U p(\theta|\mathbf{x})\,d\theta\,=\,1-\alpha}{\arg\min}\;(U - L).
\end{equation}
An equivalent characterisation: $[L^*, U^*]$ is the unique interval for which
$p(L^* \mid \mathbf{x}) = p(U^* \mid \mathbf{x})$, so every point inside has posterior
density at least as high as every point outside (hence ``highest density'').

For symmetric, unimodal posteriors the HDI and ETI coincide. They diverge for skewed
distributions — for instance, a Beta posterior near $\theta = 0$ or $\theta = 1$ — where
the HDI is noticeably shorter.

\subsection*{Numerical Computation}

The HDI is computed numerically by minimising the interval width over the quantile
function (see \texttt{HDIofICDF} in the accompanying code):
\begin{equation}
  \min_{\ell \in [0,\,\alpha]}\left[F^{-1}(1-\alpha+\ell) - F^{-1}(\ell)\right].
\end{equation}
The minimising $\ell^*$ gives $L^* = F^{-1}(\ell^*)$ and $U^* = F^{-1}(1-\alpha+\ell^*)$.
For the Binomial model used in this work the posterior is $\mathrm{Beta}(s+1,\,f+1)$,
where $s$ and $f$ are the observed successes and failures respectively; the HDI
is found by applying the above minimisation to the Beta quantile function.
