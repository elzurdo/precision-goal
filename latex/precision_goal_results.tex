We begin with a detailed examination of the fair coin case $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
to establish the strengths and limitations of each algorithm,
using $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ and precision goal $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$.
We then present general trends across $\theta_{\rm true}$ values from $0.5$ upwards,
spanning the ROPE and its boundaries.
While our primary analysis uses $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ and $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$,
we note that examination of other parameter values confirms these findings.

\subsection{General Trends Across $\theta_{\rm true}$ Values}\label{sec:trends}

$\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ upwards.\footnote{Due to symmetry of the problem the results between $0.5$ downwards would mirror the ones shown here.}

Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} illustrates the conclusiveness rates of each
algorithm (left panel), the acceptance and rejections rates (middle panel) as well
as the decision correctness (right panel) where the horizontal axis is the true success rate $\theta_{\rm true}$ and the null hypothesis
is $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$.
The ROPE top boundary is highlighted with vertical dashed lines to indicate an important inflection point.

As detailed in Section \ref{sec:fair_coin} the conclusiveness rate of HDI+ROPE (red)
and ePitG (green) both start at near 100\% for $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, where PitG (blue)
is at 36.7\%. Note that these results are a manifestation of the current setting with
$\omega_{\rm goal}=0.8$. If, e.g, we use a more stringent goal of $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.6$
the conclusiveness at  $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ of PitG increases drastically to 82.5\%
at the expense of substantially larger $N_{\rm goal}=1065$ (1.8 larger). That said,
the general trends seen here may be replicated to different extents for varying values of $\omega_{\rm goal}$.

In the left panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} the conclusiveness of all
the algorithms decrease as $\theta_{\rm true}$ nears the ROPE boundary
which serves as a minimal point afterwhich they rise again. Whereas HDI+ROPE and ePitG
return to near 100\% at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.6$, PitG assymptotes to 100\% only at
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.64$ at which ePitG and PitG are effectively the same algorithm
(assuming the fair coin case of $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$).

In the middle panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} the conclusiveness rate
is broken down to its two components: the acceptence  and rejection rates (their sum at
each point is the conclusiveness rate). In this case of $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ accepting the null hypothesis at
$\theta_{\rm true} \le 0.55$ is correct and rejecting is incorrect and vice versa for
$\theta_{\rm true} > 0.55$. As seen in Section \ref{sec:fair_coin}
the HDI+ROPE starts with a false positive/rejection rate of $\sim 6\%$ at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
(manifested in the middle panel as the red dashed line in the middle panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}) and reaches a max of $\sim 30\%$
at the inflection point. Examining its acceptence rate (red solid line) we
see that at the inflection point it has $\sim$10\% false negative/acceptance rate but quickly drops to the correct 0\%
by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$. As seen above both of the precision based methods start at zero false positives/rejections at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$,
and there is a small rise to the inflection point at which both have small false negative/acceptence rates that similarly drop quickly.

Due to the nature of the ROPE setup the its boundaries are inflection points for the algorithms,
as they are designed to accept $\theta_{\rm null}$ within the ROPE and reject it outside the ROPE.
The precision based methods (PitG and ePitG) are designed to be more conservative around the ROPE boundaries,
which is reflected in their lower conclusiveness rates compared to HDI+ROPE in this region.
If $\theta_{\rm true}$ is equal to the ROPE boundary (or fairly close) the ePitG method shown
here is only 19\% likely to be conclusive whereas the PitG is only 5\% likely.

In the right panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} we address the issue of
experiements with inconclusive decisions.
For each inconclusive experiment (i.e, when an algorithm determines "inconclusiveness" when
its stop criterion is met) one needs to assign a decision of acceptance or rejection of
$\theta_{\rm null}$. We defer an examination of different types of risk or utility based decisions
and apply here a simplistic, and we believe, a popular method. We assign the decision
based on the mean of the posterior ($\hat{\theta}=N_{\rm success}/N_{\rm total}$)
is inside or outside the ROPE, respectively. This is a common approach to dealing with inconclusive experiments, but it is important
to note that it is not the only one and it may not be the most appropriate in all cases.
For the conclusive experiments we keep the decisions as they are.
At each $\theta_{\rm true}$ value we then calculate the overall acceptence and rejection
rates for all 2,000 experiments (conclusive and inconclusive) and then flag them as
\textit{correct} or \textit{incorrect} by comparing them to the ROPE boundaries (i.e, True/False positives/negatives).
Finally the \textit{Decision Correctness Rates} (This is effectively what is called Accuracy, right?) are calculated as the proportion of correct decisions (accept or reject) out of all decisions (correct and incorrect) for each $\theta_{\rm true}$ value.

\subsection{Application to Other Data Types and Tests}

While this section focused on one-group binomial experiments,
the ePitG method is designed to be general and applicable to a wide range of data types
and statistical tests. The key principle of ePitG is to combine precision-based stopping
criteria with a requirement for the HDI to fall fully within the ROPE, which can be adapted
to various contexts.
