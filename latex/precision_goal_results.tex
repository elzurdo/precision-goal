We begin with a detailed examination of the fair coin case $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
to establish the strengths and limitations of each algorithm,
using $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ and precision goal $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$.
We then present general trends across $\theta_{\rm true}$ values from $0.5$ upwards,
spanning the ROPE and its boundaries.
While our primary analysis uses $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ and $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$,
we note that examination of other parameter values confirms these findings.

\subsection{General Trends Across $\theta_{\rm true}$ Values}\label{sec:trends}

Building on the fair coin baseline, we now examine how all three algorithms perform as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ increases from $0.5$ upwards, spanning the ROPE and its
boundaries.\footnote{By symmetry, results for $\theta_{\rm true}<0.5$ mirror those shown
here.}

Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} shows how all three algorithms perform as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ varies across and beyond the ROPE, with ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$
serving as a key inflection point in all three panels.

As established in Section~\ref{sec:fair_coin}, HDI+ROPE and ePitG open near 100\%
conclusiveness at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, while PitG sits at only 36.7\%. We have
verified that these qualitative trends generalise across values of
$\omega_{\rm goal}$.\footnote{For example, tightening to $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$ raises
PitG's conclusiveness at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ to 82.5\%, but at the cost of a
substantially larger $N_{\rm goal}=1{,}065$ --- a $1.8\times$ increase.}

All three algorithms show a dip in conclusiveness as $\theta_{\rm true}$ approaches
${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, after which conclusiveness rises again. HDI+ROPE and ePitG
recover to near 100\% by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.6$; PitG only asymptotes to 100\% at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.64$, at which point ePitG and PitG become effectively
equivalent (for $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$).

This dip is by design: the ROPE boundaries are natural inflection points, as algorithms
are constructed to accept $\theta_{\rm null}$ within the ROPE and reject it outside.
The precision-based methods are deliberately conservative near these boundaries, yielding
lower conclusiveness rates than HDI+ROPE in this region. At the boundary itself, ePitG
is conclusive in only 19\% of experiments and PitG in only 5\%.

Breaking the conclusiveness into its acceptance and rejection components (middle panel)
reveals the error structure. For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, acceptance is the correct
decision within the ROPE ($\theta_{\rm true}\le 0.55$) and rejection is correct outside
it. HDI+ROPE's false positive rate (i.e, rejecting $\theta_{\rm null}$ when it is true) of $\sim\!6\%$ at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ climbs to
$\sim\!30\%$ at ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$; its false negative rate (i.e, accepting $\theta_{\rm null}$ when it is false)
peaks at $\sim\!10\%$ at
the boundary before dropping to 0\% by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$. Both precision-based
methods maintain zero false positives throughout, with only a small false negative rate
near the ROPE boundary that similarly drops quickly. %% that peaks at the ROPE boundary and drops to zero by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$.

The right panel shows overall \textit{accuracy}, the proportion of correct decisions
across all $M$ experiments, after assigning each inconclusive experiment a decision
based on whether $\hat{\theta}$ lies within the ROPE (accept) or outside it (reject).
Alternative risk- or utility-based assignment rules exist but are deferred to future
work.

All three algorithms reach their minimum accuracy at ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$. Within
the ROPE, ePitG consistently leads PitG, reaching 99.5\% at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
compared to PitG's 98.5\%. Outside the ROPE, HDI+ROPE and ePitG reach 100\% by
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$, whereas PitG achieves this only at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.60$.

That HDI+ROPE reaches 100\% accuracy at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$ while simultaneously
producing biased $\hat{\theta}$ estimates motivates a closer look at the observed
success rate $\hat{\theta}$ statistics. Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive} shows, for conclusive
experiments, the IQR of $\hat{\theta}(N_{\rm stop})$ (left panel) and the median bias
$\hat{\theta}_{\rm 50\%}-\theta_{\rm true}$ (right panel).\footnote{We find similar
trends for the mean, but omit it to reduce clutter.}

Before examining per-algorithm bias, it is worth recalling what an accept or reject
decision means in this framework. Following \cite{kruschke2018}, accepting
$\theta_{\rm null}$ for practical purposes does not mean that $\theta_{\rm null}$ is
itself the most credible value of the posterior; it means merely that \textit{all} the
most credible values---the entire HDI---are practically equivalent to $\theta_{\rm null}$.
The relevant quantity for an accept/reject decision is therefore the posterior HDI, not
the point estimate $\hat{\theta}$. A small bias in $\hat{\theta}$ does not invalidate
the decision so long as the HDI falls cleanly within the ROPE.

With this in mind, the $\hat{\theta}$ bias of HDI+ROPE is more telling as a symptom
of its lack of a precision requirement than as a decision-quality metric. At
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, all three algorithms show near-zero bias, as expected from
the symmetry of the fair coin case. Once $\theta_{\rm true}$ deviates from $0.5$,
HDI+ROPE's bias grows markedly: its median bias approaches the ROPE half-width at
${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, with the upper IQR extending substantially further.
This happens because HDI+ROPE can stop on skewed samples before sufficient data
have accumulated to constrain the posterior.

The precision-based methods exhibit substantially smaller $\hat{\theta}$ bias, well
within the ROPE width across all $\theta_{\rm true}$ values, as a natural consequence
of their precision requirement. Among conclusive experiments within the ROPE, PitG
shows the highest bias and ePitG the lowest.\footnote{For inconclusive experiments,
where PitG contributes far more experiments than ePitG or HDI+ROPE, PitG's
$\hat{\theta}$ bias is slightly \textit{lower} than ePitG's, though this ordering
reverses when compounding all experiments.} Compounding conclusive and inconclusive
experiments, ePitG accuracy exceeds PitG's at every $\theta_{\rm true}$ value
(cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}, right panel).


The preceding analysis has shown that ePitG is decisively superior to PitG
near and within the ROPE, at the cost of a larger median $N_{\rm stop}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:stop_iterations_by_truth_conclusive} quantifies this cost by showing
$N_{\rm stop}$ statistics for conclusive experiments across $\theta_{\rm true}$,
restricted to conclusive outcomes since the stop iteration of inconclusive experiments
is not informative. The left column uses $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ (the fair coin setup
of Section~\ref{sec:fair_coin}); the right uses $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$ to demonstrate
generality. In both columns the dotted curve is the theoretical minimum stop iteration
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true}, \omega_{\rm goal})$ (Equation~\ref{eq:pitg_stop_iteration}),
which reflects what nature dictates given the true parameter. The horizontal dashed line
is $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null}, \omega_{\rm goal})$, the sample size a researcher
would plan in advance knowing only $\theta_{\rm null}$.

HDI+ROPE (top panels) shows distinctly different behaviour inside and outside the ROPE.
Within the ROPE, $N_{\rm stop}$ loosely tracks $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$, but
with a wide IQR reflecting the absence of a precision criterion. Outside the ROPE,
$N_{\rm stop}$ falls well below $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$: HDI+ROPE correctly
rejects $\theta_{\rm null}$ much faster than precision-based methods, but does so on
the basis of imprecise posteriors, producing the strong $\hat{\theta}$ bias documented
in Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive}.

PitG (middle panels) stops tightly around $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$ by design,
making it the natural reference for understanding the precision cost. The contrast with
the planned quantity $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ (horizontal dashed line) reveals
an important asymmetry. Because $N_{\rm goal}(\theta)$ is maximised at $\theta=0.5$ and
decreases as $\theta$ moves towards $0$ or $1$, the direction of the gap between the
two quantities depends on where $\theta_{\rm true}$ lies relative to $\theta_{\rm null}$.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ (left panels), any deviation of $\theta_{\rm true}$ from
$0.5$ implies $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true}) < N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$:
the researcher's planned budget of 599 samples is a conservative upper bound, and
experiments stop earlier whenever the truth differs from the null.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$ (right panels), the picture is asymmetric. When
$\theta_{\rm true}>\theta_{\rm null}$ (further from $0.5$), $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})<
N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ and experiments again stop sooner than planned.
When $\theta_{\rm true}<\theta_{\rm null}$ (closer to $0.5$), however,
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})>N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null}=0.7)\approx 503$:
the researcher's planned budget underestimates the samples required, and
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ should be treated as a lower bound rather than
an upper one. In practice, the researcher should plan a budget floor of
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ and set $N_{\rm max}$ according to their resources
(here $N_{\rm max}=1{,}500$).

ePitG (bottom panels) has $N_{\rm stop}$ equal to or larger than PitG's within
the ROPE and in the vicinity of its boundaries, the extra iterations being precisely
what purchases the higher conclusiveness seen in Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}.
Once $\theta_{\rm true}$ is far enough from the ROPE, ePitG converges to PitG and
both stop at $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$.

The conclusiveness ratio substantially exceeds the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio throughout,
particularly within the ROPE and immediately outside it. Strikingly, the conclusiveness
ratio remains above 1 even where the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio equals 1---that is, where both
algorithms stop at the same iteration---which occurs for roughly

$\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$ (not shown).


\subsection{Application to Other Data Types and Tests}

While this section focused on one-group binomial experiments,
the ePitG method is designed to be general and applicable to a wide range of data types
and statistical tests. The key principle of ePitG is to combine precision-based stopping
criteria with a requirement for the HDI to fall fully within the ROPE, which can be adapted
to various contexts.
