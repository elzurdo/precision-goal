We begin with a detailed examination of the fair coin case $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
to establish the strengths and limitations of each algorithm.
We then present general trends across $\theta_{\rm true}$ values from $0.5$ upwards,
spanning the ROPE and its boundaries.

\subsection{Fair Coin ($\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$)}\label{sec:fair_coin}

Figure \ref{fig:iterations} presents a hand-picked fair coin experiment chosen to
illustrate stark differences in outcomes across the three algorithms.\footnote{This figure
is similar to the top panels in \cite{kruschke2015doing} Figures 13.4 and 13.5.
The sequence itself may be found in the open source material under zurda bah SEQUENCE\_HANDPICKED.
\textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TODO: provide the exact sequence]}}}
The posteriors in Figure~\ref{fig:posteriors} correspond to three specific iterations
of this same experiment.

Despite arising from the same sequence of coin tosses, the three algorithms reach
sharply different conclusions. HDI+ROPE stops early and confidently, though incorrectly,
rejects $\theta_{\rm null}$. PitG stops at the expected precision iteration but is left
inconclusive, as the HDI straddles the ROPE boundary. ePitG waits until both conditions
are jointly satisfied and correctly accepts $\theta_{\rm null}$. This divergence from a
single experiment motivates the large-scale analysis that follows.

In Figure ~\ref{fig:fair_iter_vs_rate} we see that HDI+ROPE yields a wide range of stop iterations and sample proportions $\hat{\theta}$, with a
significant portion of results falling outside the ROPE, leading to incorrect rejections
of $\theta_{\rm null}$. Its $N_{\rm stop}$ distribution is skewed towards early stopping
at $\sim\!400$, well before $N_{\rm goal}\sim 600$, with a long tail extending to later
iterations.

PitG consistently stops near $N_{\rm goal}$, with sample proportions clustered tightly around
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$. However, many of these stops occur when the HDI straddles the
ROPE boundary, yielding a high rate of inconclusive decisions
(see Figure~\ref{fig:fair_decisions} and Table~\ref{tab:fair_overall}).
ePitG exhibits a narrower $\hat{\theta}(N_{\rm stop})$ distribution than PitG but extends
to later iterations, achieving more conclusive results while maintaining unbiased estimates.

As seen in Figure~\ref{fig:fair_decisions}, HDI+ROPE's rejection rate (left panel) quickly asymptotes at 6\%, while its acceptance
rate begins rising near $N\sim 400$, reflecting a gradual shift from incorrect rejections
to correct acceptances as more data accumulate.

Both precision-based methods maintain a zero rejection rate (middle and right panels),
as they require precision criteria to be satisfied before any decision is made.
For PitG (middle panel), the acceptance rate rises as a near step-function, jumping at
$N_{\rm goal}$ to 36.7\%, with the inconclusive rate settling at the complement 63.3\%.

ePitG (right panel) presents a hybrid of the other two: it preserves the zero-rejection
property of PitG while achieving a high conclusiveness rate akin to HDI+ROPE, but with
a more gradual transition from inconclusive to conclusive outcomes.

The choice of $\omega_{\rm goal}$ governs the fundamental precision--conclusiveness
trade-off. Tightening from $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$ to $0.06$ raises PitG's
conclusiveness at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ from 36.7\% to 82.5\%, and ePitG's from
97.7\% to \textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TODO: verify ePitG conclusiveness at
$\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$, placeholder $\sim\!99\%$]}}, at the cost of a substantially
larger required sample size: $N_{\rm goal}=1{,}065$, a $1.8\times$ increase.
Practitioners should therefore select $\omega_{\rm goal}$ to reflect their tolerance
for imprecision and their available sampling budget.

The fair coin case thus demonstrates a clear hierarchy: ePitG matches HDI+ROPE's
conclusiveness and PitG's zero-false-positive guarantee at a modest median cost.
Section~\ref{sec:trends} shows that this advantage persists as $\theta_{\rm true}$
departs from $\theta_{\rm null}$.

\subsection{General Trends Across $\theta_{\rm true}$ Values}\label{sec:trends}

Building on the fair coin baseline, we now examine how all three algorithms perform as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ increases from $0.5$ upwards, spanning the ROPE and its
boundaries.\footnote{By symmetry, results for $\theta_{\rm true}<0.5$ mirror those shown
here, as reflected in Equation~\ref{eq:pitg_stop_iteration} and Figure~\ref{fig:min_sample_by_goal}.}

Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} shows how all three algorithms perform as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ varies across and beyond the ROPE, with ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$
serving as a key inflection point in all three panels.

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{conclusive_rates.png}
  \caption{Algorithm comparison across $\theta_{\rm true}$ values for $M=2{,}000$
  experiments ($\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$).
  \textit{Colors}: red: HDI+ROPE; blue: PitG; green: ePitG.
  \textit{Vertical dashed lines}: ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$.
  \textit{Left}: conclusiveness rates.
  \textit{Middle}: acceptance (solid lines) and rejection (dashed lines) rates;
  at each $\theta_{\rm true}$ their sum equals the respective conclusiveness rate.
  \textit{Right}: accuracy rates (proportion of correct decisions out of all
  $M$ experiments); inconclusive experiments are assigned a decision based on whether
  $\hat{\theta}$ falls inside or outside the ROPE
  (cf.\ Section~\ref{sec:trends}).}
  \label{fig:conclusiveness_rates}
\end{figure}

As established in Section~\ref{sec:fair_coin}, HDI+ROPE and ePitG open near 100\%
conclusiveness at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, while PitG sits at only 36.7\%, with the
trade-off against $\omega_{\rm goal}$ discussed there. The qualitative trends shown
below generalise across values of $\omega_{\rm goal}$.

All three algorithms show a dip in conclusiveness as $\theta_{\rm true}$ approaches
${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, after which conclusiveness rises again. HDI+ROPE and ePitG
recover to near 100\% by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.6$; PitG only asymptotes to 100\% at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.64$, at which point ePitG and PitG become effectively
equivalent (for $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$).

This dip is by design: the ROPE boundaries are natural inflection points, as algorithms
are constructed to accept $\theta_{\rm null}$ within the ROPE and reject it outside 
(see Section~\ref{sec:accept_reject_meaning}).
The precision-based methods are deliberately conservative near these boundaries, yielding
lower conclusiveness rates than HDI+ROPE in this region. At the boundary itself, ePitG
is conclusive in only 19\% of experiments and PitG in only 5\%.

Breaking the conclusiveness into its acceptance and rejection components (middle panel)
reveals the error structure. For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, acceptance is the correct
decision within the ROPE ($\theta_{\rm true}\le 0.55$) (i.e, within the ROPE) and rejection is correct outside
it. HDI+ROPE's false positive rate (i.e., rejecting $\theta_{\rm null}$ when $\theta_{\rm true}$ is within the ROPE)
climbs from $\sim\!6\%$ at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ to $\sim\!30\%$ at
${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$; its false negative rate (i.e., accepting the null when $\theta_{\rm true}$ is outside the ROPE)
peaks at $\sim\!10\%$ at the ROPE boundary before dropping to $0\%$
by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$. Both precision-based methods maintain a near-zero
false-positive rate throughout, exactly zero well within the ROPE interior, rising to a
small level near the ROPE boundary (ePitG at the same $\sim\!10\%$, PitG lower due to
its much lower conclusiveness rate), with only a small false negative rate near the ROPE
that similarly drops quickly.

The right panel shows overall \textit{accuracy}, the proportion of correct decisions
across all $M$ experiments. Since the three algorithms have different conclusiveness
rates, each inconclusive experiment is assigned a decision based on whether
$\hat{\theta}$ lies within the ROPE (accept) or outside it (reject); alternative
risk- or utility-based rules are deferred to future work.

All three algorithms reach their minimum accuracy at ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$. Within
the ROPE, ePitG consistently leads PitG, reaching 99.5\% at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
compared to PitG's 98.5\%. Outside the ROPE, HDI+ROPE and ePitG reach 100\% by
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$, whereas PitG achieves this only at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.60$.

That HDI+ROPE reaches 100\% accuracy at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$ while simultaneously
producing biased $\hat{\theta}$ estimates motivates a closer look at the observed
sample proportion statistics. Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive} shows, for
conclusive experiments, the IQR of $\hat{\theta}(N_{\rm stop})$ (left panel) and the
median bias $\hat{\theta}_{\rm 50\%}-\theta_{\rm true}$ (right panel).\footnote{We find
similar trends for the mean, but omit it to reduce clutter.}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:accept_reject_meaning}, accept/reject decisions rest on
the HDI's position relative to the ROPE, not on $\hat{\theta}$; a bias in $\hat{\theta}$
does not invalidate the decision so long as the HDI falls cleanly within (or outside)
the ROPE.

HDI+ROPE's $\hat{\theta}$ bias is therefore more telling as a symptom of its lack of a
precision requirement than as a decision-quality metric. At $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$,
all three algorithms show near-zero bias, as expected from the symmetry of the fair coin
case. Once $\theta_{\rm true}$ deviates from $0.5$, HDI+ROPE's bias grows markedly: its
median bias approaches ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, with the upper
IQR extending substantially further---because HDI+ROPE can stop on skewed samples before
sufficient data have accumulated to constrain the posterior.

The precision-based methods exhibit substantially smaller $\hat{\theta}$ bias, well
within the ROPE width across all $\theta_{\rm true}$ values, as a natural consequence
of their precision requirement. Among conclusive experiments within the ROPE, PitG
shows the highest bias and ePitG the lowest.\footnote{For inconclusive experiments,
where PitG contributes far more experiments than ePitG or HDI+ROPE, PitG's
$\hat{\theta}$ bias is slightly \textit{lower} than ePitG's, though this ordering
reverses when compounding all experiments.} Compounding conclusive and inconclusive
experiments, ePitG decision accuracy exceeds PitG's at every $\theta_{\rm true}$ value
(cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}, right panel).


The preceding analysis has shown that ePitG is decisively superior to PitG
near and within the ROPE, at the cost of a larger median $N_{\rm stop}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:stop_iterations_by_truth_conclusive} quantifies this cost for all three
algorithms, restricting to conclusive outcomes since the stop iteration of inconclusive
experiments is not informative; including HDI+ROPE also illustrates why the precision
requirement matters.

HDI+ROPE shows distinctly different behaviour inside and outside the ROPE.
Within the ROPE, $N_{\rm stop}$ loosely tracks $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$, but
with a wide IQR reflecting the absence of a precision criterion. Outside the ROPE,
$N_{\rm stop}$ falls well below $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$: HDI+ROPE correctly
rejects $\theta_{\rm null}$ much faster than precision-based methods, but does so on
the basis of imprecise posteriors, producing the strong $\hat{\theta}$ bias seen in
cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive}.

PitG stops tightly around $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$ by design, making it the
natural reference for understanding the precision cost. The contrast with the planned
quantity $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ reveals an important asymmetry: because
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta)$ is maximised at $\theta=0.5$ and decreases as $\theta$ moves
towards $0$ or $1$, the direction of the gap depends on where $\theta_{\rm true}$ lies
relative to $\theta_{\rm null}$.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, any deviation of $\theta_{\rm true}$ from $0.5$ implies
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true}) < N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$: the planned budget
of 599 samples is a conservative upper bound, and experiments stop earlier whenever
the truth differs from the null.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$, the picture is asymmetric. When $\theta_{\rm true}>
\theta_{\rm null}$ (further from $0.5$), $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})<
N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ and experiments stop sooner than planned. When
$\theta_{\rm true}<\theta_{\rm null}$ (closer to $0.5$), however,
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})>N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null}=0.7)\approx 503$:
the planned budget underestimates the samples required, and
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ acts as a lower bound rather than an upper one.

In general, $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ serves as the researcher's advance
planning estimate: a conservative upper bound when $\theta_{\rm null}$ lies at the
precision maximum (e.g.\ $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$), and a floor otherwise.
The researcher should set $N_{\rm max}$ according to their resources, with
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ as the baseline (here $N_{\rm max}=1{,}500$).

ePitG's $N_{\rm stop}$ equals or exceeds PitG's within the ROPE and in the vicinity
of its boundaries, the extra iterations being precisely what accounts for the higher
conclusiveness seen in Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}. Once $\theta_{\rm true}$
is far enough from the ROPE, ePitG converges to PitG and both stop at
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$.

Figure~\ref{fig:stop_conclusiveness_ratios} places the ePitG--PitG trade-off in direct
comparison by overlaying the ratio of conclusiveness rates
(from Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}) and the ratio of $N_{\rm stop}$
(from Figure~\ref{fig:stop_iterations_by_truth_conclusive}) across $\theta_{\rm true}$.

The conclusiveness ratio substantially exceeds the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio throughout,
particularly within the ROPE and immediately outside it. Strikingly, the conclusiveness
ratio remains above 1 even where the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio equals 1---that is, where both
algorithms stop at the same iteration---which occurs for roughly
$0.60 \lesssim \theta_{\rm true} \lesssim 0.63$: ePitG gains additional conclusive outcomes
at no extra sampling cost in this region. The lower band of the IQR confirms that a
substantial fraction of ePitG experiments stop exactly when PitG does, keeping the
median $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio low. At the fair coin value $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$,
the conclusiveness ratio is $2.66$, while the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio has an IQR of
$1.00$--$1.29$ (median $1.04$, mean $1.21$): roughly a $2.7\times$ gain in
conclusiveness at a median cost of only $4\%$ more samples.

The apparent flattening of both ratios near ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$ is an artefact of
the finite cap $N_{\rm max}=1{,}500$; with a larger cap both curves would peak sharply
at the boundary. Qualitatively similar results hold for $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$ and
$\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$ (not shown).
