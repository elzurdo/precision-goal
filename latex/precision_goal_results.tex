\subsection{Fair Coin ($\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$)}\label{sec:fair_coin}

Figure \ref{fig:iterations} presents the results of a hand-picked fair coin experiment.\footnote{This figure is similar to the top panels in \cite{kruschke2015doing} Figures 13.4 and 13.5. \textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TODO: provide the exact sequence]}}}
It was chosen to highlight potential stark differences in outcomes between the three algorithms.
Note that the posteriors shown in Figure \ref{fig:posteriors} correspond to three specific iterations of this single experiment.

As seen in Figure \ref{fig:iterations}, at iteration 126 (marked as a vertical red dashed line), the 95\% HDI of the \textit{HDI+ROPE} algorithm falls fully outside the ROPE, causing it to confidently---though incorrectly---reject $\theta_{\rm null}$.

In contrast, the \textit{Precision is the Goal} (PitG) stop criterion is met at iteration 598 (marked as the first pair of purple dots), when the HDI width becomes narrower than the precision goal of 0.08. However, because the HDI still straddles the ROPE boundary, the decision is inconclusive.

Finally, for \textit{Enhanced Precision is the Goal} (ePitG), the stop criterion is only
met at iteration 804 (marked as the green vertical line), 
when the HDI satisfies both conditions: it is narrower than the precision goal \textit{and} falls fully within the ROPE.
This results in a correct acceptance of $\theta_{\rm null}$.

We notice that the HDI+ROPE (red squares) yields a wide range of stop iterations and success rates,
with a significant portion of results falling outside the ROPE, leading to incorrect rejections of $\theta_{\rm null}$.
Its distribution of $N$ shows both a skewness towards early stopping at $\sim 400$, much
earlier than the precision stop criterion of $N_{\theta_{\rm true}}\sim 600$ (dashed vertical line), and a long tail extending to later iterations.

In contrast, PitG (blue circles) consistently stops around iteration $N_{\theta_{\rm true}}$
with success rates clustered around $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$.
However, many of these stops occur when the HDI straddles the ROPE boundary, resulting in a high rate of inconclusive decisions (see Figure \ref{fig:fair_decisions} and Table \ref{tab:fair_overall}).
ePitG (green Xs) shows a narrower $\hat{\theta}(N)$ distribution than PitG but extends to later iterations,
achieving more conclusive results while maintaining unbiased estimates.

As this binomial setup is well-defined, we observe trends that can be described analytically. We discuss this in Section \textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TK]}}, but note here that the expected stop iteration for Precision is the Goal is approximated by:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pitg_stop_iteration}
n \approx \frac{4 z_{*}^2}{\rm{CI}^2}p(1-p) - 1
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $p$ is the success rate. \textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TK: Perhaps this should be $\theta_{\rm true}$?]}}
  \item ${\rm CI}$ is the precision goal (here 0.08).
  \item $z_{*}$ is the critical value (e.g., $z_{0.05}=1.96$). \textcolor{red}{\textbf{[TK: confirm variable. For some reason I wote "E.g, for 95\% quantile of the standard normal distribution"]}}
\end{itemize}

For this fair coin instance ($p=0.5$), we obtain $n \approx 599.2$, indicated by the dashed vertical line in Figure \ref{fig:fair_iter_vs_rate}.

\subsection{General Trends Across $\theta_{\rm true}$ Values}

We next examine trends of these algorithms when conducting the experiments from
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$ onwards.\footnote{Due to symmetry of the problem the results between $0.5$ downwards would mirror the ones shown here.}

Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} illustrates the conclusiveness rates of each
algorithm (left panel), the acceptance and rejections rates (middle panel)
where the horizontal axis is the true success rate $\theta_{\rm true}$ and the null hypothesis
is $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$.
The ROPE top boundary is highlighted with vertical dashed lines to indicate an important inflection point.

As detailed in Section \ref{sec:fair_coin} the conclusiveness rate of HDI+ROPE (red)
and ePitG (green) both start at near 100\% for $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, where PitG (blue)
at 36.7\%. In the left panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} they all decrease as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ nears the ROPE boundary
which serves as a minimal point afterwhich they rise again. Whereas HDI+ROPE and ePitG
return to near 100\% at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.6$, PitG assymptotes to 100\% only at
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.64$ at which ePitG and PitG are effectively the same algorithm
(assuming the fair coin case of $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$).

In the middle panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} the conclusiveness rate
is broken down to its two components: the acceptence  and rejection rates.
Here we similar trends. In this case of $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$ accepting the null hypothesis at
$\theta_{\rm true} \le 0.55$ is correct and rejecting is incorrect and vice versa for
$\theta_{\rm true} \ge 0.55$.

As seen in Section \ref{sec:fair_coin}
the HDI+ROPE starts with a false positive/rejection rate of $\sim 6\%$ at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
(manifested in the middle panel as the red dashed line) and reaches a max of $\sim 30\%$
at the inflection point. Examining its acceptence rate (red solid line) we
see that at the inflection point it has $\sim$10\% false negative/acceptance rate but quickly drops to the correct 0\%
by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$.

Both of the precision based methods start at zero false positives/rejections at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$,
and there is a small rise to the inflection point at which both have small false negative/acceptence rates that similarly drop quickly.

Due to the nature of the ROPE setup the its boundaries are inflection points for the algorithms,
as they are designed to accept $\theta_{\rm null}$ within the ROPE and reject it outside the ROPE.
The precision based methods (PitG and ePitG) are designed to be more conservative around the ROPE boundaries,
which is reflected in their lower conclusiveness rates compared to HDI+ROPE in this region.
If $\theta_{\rm true}$ is equal to the ROPE boundary (or fairly close) the ePitG method shown
here is only 19\% likely to be conclusive whereas the PitG is only 5\% likely.

In the right panel of Figure \ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates} we address the issue of
experiements with inconclusive decisions.
For each inconclusive experiment (i.e, when an algorithm determines "inconclusiveness" when
its stop criterion is met) we assign a decision of acceptance or rejection of
$\theta_{\rm null}$ by checking whether the mean of the posterior ($N_{\rm success}/N_{\rm total}$)
is inside or outside the ROPE, respectively. This is a common approach to dealing with inconclusive experiments, but it is important
to note that it is not the only one and it may not be the most appropriate in all cases.
For the conclusive experiments we keep the decisions as they are.
At each $\theta_{\rm true}$ values we then calculate the overall acceptence and rejection
rates for all 2,000 experiments (conclusive and inconclusive) and then flag them as \textit{correct} or \textit{incorrect} by comparing them to the ROPE boundaries.
Finally the \textit{Decision Correctness Rates} are calculated as the proportion of correct decisions (accept or reject) out of all decisions (correct and incorrect) for each $\theta_{\rm true}$ value.

The stop iteration for HDI+ROPE has different signatures depending on its relationship
to the ROPE. Within the ROPE it tends to loosely track $N_{\rm goal}$, albeit with a
wide IQR.

The PitG $N_{\rm stop}$ is tightly clustered around $N_{\rm goal}$ across all $\theta_{\rm true}$ values,

the $N_{\rm stop}$ of ePitG converges towards that of PitG,
suggesting that the additional precision criteria have less impact on
stopping decisions when the true parameter is clearly outside the ROPE.

\subsection{Application to Other Data Types and Tests}

While this section focused on one-group binomial experiments,
the ePitG method is designed to be general and applicable to a wide range of data types
and statistical tests. The key principle of ePitG is to combine precision-based stopping
criteria with a requirement for the HDI to fall fully within the ROPE, which can be adapted
to various contexts.
