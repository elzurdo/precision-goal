Sequential hypothesis testing allows data collection to continue until sufficient evidence
accumulates, offering flexibility over fixed-sample designs.
A key challenge, however, is that stopping rules which trigger on decision criteria,
such as p-value thresholds or posterior-based heuristics, can introduce confirmation
bias through early peeking on unrepresentative samples.
Methods that decouple the stopping criterion from the decision criterion, such as
``Precision is the Goal'' (PitG), eliminate this bias by halting only once a target
posterior precision is reached.
Yet we show that PitG frequently yields inconclusive outcomes, most notably when the
null hypothesis is true.
To address this, we propose ``Enhanced Precision is the Goal'' (ePitG), which requires
both the precision target and a conclusive hypothesis outcome to be met simultaneously
before stopping.
We demonstrate that ePitG substantially increases decisiveness while preserving PitG's
zero-false-positive property, at only a modest cost in median sample size.
In fair coin experiments ($\theta_{\rm true}= \theta_{\rm null}=0.5$) with a precision goal of
$\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$, PitG yields 63.3\% inconclusive outcomes; ePitG reduces this
to just 2.3\%, with only a 4.7\% increase in median sample size.
More broadly, unlike methods that can only reject the null hypothesis, the
precision-based framework also permits its positive acceptance,
a guarantee it preserves with zero false positives.
We provide an online interactive calculator and open-source code to support the planning
and interpretation of sequential experiments.
