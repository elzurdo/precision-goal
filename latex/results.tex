Across all scenarios, DPitG substantially outperforms PitG in conclusiveness while
keeping false-positive rates well below HDI+ROPE's, at a modest median sampling premium.
We begin with the fair coin case ($\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$), where DPitG matches
HDI+ROPE's near-complete conclusiveness while, like PitG, producing zero false
positives --- a combination neither competitor achieves alone.
We then examine general trends as $\theta_{\rm true}$ increases from $0.5$ upwards,
across and beyond the ROPE, including the dip in conclusiveness near the ROPE boundary
that both precision-based methods exhibit by design.

\subsection{Fair Coin ($\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$)}\label{sec:fair_coin}

\subsubsection{A Hand-Picked Sequence}\label{sec:fair_coin_sequence}

Figure~\ref{fig:iterations} presents a hand-picked fair coin experiment chosen to
illustrate stark differences in outcomes across the three algorithms.\footnote{This figure
is similar to the top panels in \cite{kruschke2015doing} Figures 13.4 and 13.5.
The sequence itself may be found in the open source material as 
\href{https://github.com/elzurdo/precision-goal/blob/main/py/utils_experiments.py\#L28}{\texttt{SEQUENCE\_HANDPICKED}}.}
The posteriors in Figure~\ref{fig:posteriors} correspond to three specific iterations
of this same experiment.

Despite arising from the same sequence of coin tosses, the three algorithms reach
sharply different decisions. HDI+ROPE stops early and, incorrectly, rejects
$\theta_{\rm null}$. PitG stops at the expected precision iteration but is left
inconclusive, as the HDI straddles the ROPE boundary. DPitG waits until both conditions
are jointly satisfied and correctly accepts $\theta_{\rm null}$. This divergence from a
single experiment motivates the large-scale analysis that follows.

\subsubsection{Sensitivity to $\omega_{\rm goal}$}\label{sec:goal_impact}

The choice of $\omega_{\rm goal}$ governs the fundamental precision--conclusiveness
trade-off for PitG. Table~\ref{tab:goal_impact} shows the effect across a range of values
at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$. Tightening $\omega_{\rm goal}$ (decreasing it) raises PitG's
conclusiveness at the cost of a larger required sample size: $N_{\rm goal}$ grows
quadratically as $\omega_{\rm goal}$ decreases (Equation~\ref{eq:pitg_stop_iteration}).
DPitG's conclusiveness, by contrast, remains essentially flat across the full range
($0.972$--$0.979$), demonstrating robustness to the choice of $\omega_{\rm goal}$.
The conclusiveness ratio (DPitG/PitG) captures this contrast directly: it grows from
$1.17$ at $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$ becoming undefined at $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.10$, where PitG
becomes entirely inconclusive. This comes at the cost of larger sample sizes, quantified
by the $N_{\rm 50\%}/N_{\rm goal}$ column: for $\omega_{\rm goal} \le 0.07$, the median
DPitG stop is exactly $N_{\rm goal}$ (ratio $= 1.00$); for looser goals the ratio rises
from $1.04$ at $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$ to $1.41$ at $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.10$.

At the tightest setting ($\omega_{\rm goal}=0.06$), the DPitG $N_{\rm stop}$ $\Delta_{\rm IQR}$ is zero:
all experiments stop at exactly $N_{\rm goal}$, leaving no room for the additional
iterations that DPitG normally uses to resolve borderline cases.
At the loosest setting ($\omega_{\rm goal}=0.10=\Delta_{\rm ROPE}$, equal to the ROPE width), PitG is always
inconclusive, but DPitG still achieves 97.9\% conclusiveness by collecting more data until
both precision and conclusiveness are jointly satisfied.

The conclusiveness of DPitG is more sensitive to $N_{\rm max}$ (fixed at $1{,}500$ in this
example) than to $\omega_{\rm goal}$, whereas PitG always stops at $N_{\rm goal}$ regardless of $N_{\rm max}$.
Consequently, $N_{\rm goal}$ serves as a minimum budget for DPitG: for tight precision goals
it is sufficient to achieve high conclusiveness; for looser goals, the researcher should
set $N_{\rm max}$ generously to allow DPitG to reach its full potential.

The fair coin case thus demonstrates a clear hierarchy: DPitG matches HDI+ROPE's

Section~\ref{sec:trends} shows that this advantage persists as $\theta_{\rm true}$

\subsection{General Trends Across $\theta_{\rm true}$ Values}\label{sec:trends}

Building on the fair coin baseline, we now examine how all three algorithms perform as
$\theta_{\rm true}$ increases from $0.5$ upwards, across and beyond the ROPE,\footnote{By symmetry, results for $\theta_{\rm true}<0.5$ mirror those shown
here, as reflected in Equation~\ref{eq:pitg_stop_iteration} and Figure~\ref{fig:min_sample_by_goal}.}
with ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$ serving as a key inflection point.

\begin{figure}[h!]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{conclusive_rates.png}
  \caption{Algorithm comparison across $\theta_{\rm true}$ values for $M=2{,}000$
  experiments ($\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, $\omega_{\rm goal}=0.08$).
  \textit{Colors}: red: HDI+ROPE; blue: PitG; green: DPitG.
  \textit{Vertical dashed lines}: ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$.
  \textit{Left}: conclusiveness rates.
  \textit{Middle}: acceptance (solid lines) and rejection (dashed lines) rates;
  at each $\theta_{\rm true}$ their sum equals the respective conclusiveness rate.
  \textit{Right}: accuracy rates (proportion of correct decisions out of all
  $M$ experiments); inconclusive experiments are assigned a decision based on whether
  $\hat{\theta}$ falls inside or outside the ROPE.}
  \label{fig:conclusiveness_rates}
\end{figure}

As established in Section~\ref{sec:fair_coin_large_scale}, HDI+ROPE and DPitG open near 100\%
conclusiveness at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, while PitG sits at only 36.7\%, with the
trade-off against $\omega_{\rm goal}$ discussed there. The qualitative trends shown
below generalise across values of $\omega_{\rm goal}$ (Section~\ref{sec:goal_impact}).

All three algorithms show a dip in conclusiveness as $\theta_{\rm true}$ approaches
${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, after which conclusiveness rises again. HDI+ROPE and DPitG
recover to near 100\% by $\theta_{\rm true}=0.6$; PitG only asymptotes to 100\% at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.64$, at which point DPitG and PitG become effectively
equivalent (for $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$).

This dip is by design: the ROPE boundaries are natural inflection points, as algorithms
are constructed to accept $\theta_{\rm null}$ within the ROPE and reject it outside
(see Section~\ref{sec:accept_reject_meaning}).
The precision-based methods are deliberately conservative near these boundaries, yielding
lower conclusiveness rates than HDI+ROPE in this region. At the boundary itself, DPitG
is conclusive in only 19\% of experiments and PitG in only 5\%.

Breaking the conclusiveness into its acceptance and rejection components (middle panel)
reveals the error structure.
For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, acceptance is the correct decision within the ROPE
($\theta_{\rm true}\le 0.55$) and rejection is correct outside it.
HDI+ROPE's false-positive rate climbs from $\sim\!6\%$ at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
to $\sim\!30\%$ at ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$; its false-negative rate peaks at
$\sim\!10\%$ at the ROPE boundary before dropping to $0\%$ by
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.57$. Both precision-based methods maintain a near-zero
false-positive rate throughout, exactly zero well within the ROPE interior, rising to a
small level near the ROPE boundary (DPitG at the same $\sim\!10\%$, PitG lower due to
its much lower conclusiveness rate), with only a small false-negative rate near the ROPE
that similarly drops quickly.

The right panel shows overall \textit{accuracy}, the proportion of correct decisions
across all $M$ experiments. Since the three algorithms have different conclusiveness
rates, each inconclusive experiment is assigned a decision based on whether
$\hat{\theta}$ lies within the ROPE (accept) or outside it (reject); alternative
risk- or utility-based rules are deferred to future work.

All three algorithms reach their minimum accuracy at ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$. Within
the ROPE, DPitG consistently leads PitG, reaching 99.5\% at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$
compared to PitG's 98.5\%. Outside the ROPE, HDI+ROPE and DPitG reach 100\% by
$\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$, whereas PitG achieves this only at
$\theta_{\rm true}\approx 0.60$.

That HDI+ROPE reaches 100\% accuracy at $\theta_{\rm true}=0.58$ despite
producing biased $\hat{\theta}$ estimates motivates a closer look at the observed
sample proportion statistics. Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive} shows, for
conclusive experiments, the IQR of $\hat{\theta}(N_{\rm stop})$ (left panel) and the
median bias $\hat{\theta}_{\rm 50\%}-\theta_{\rm true}$ (right panel).\footnote{We find
similar trends for the mean, but omit it to reduce clutter.}

As discussed in Section~\ref{sec:accept_reject_meaning}, accept/reject decisions rest on
the HDI's position relative to the ROPE, not on $\hat{\theta}$; a bias in $\hat{\theta}$
does not invalidate the decision so long as the HDI falls cleanly within (or outside)
the ROPE.

HDI+ROPE's $\hat{\theta}$ bias is therefore more telling as a symptom of its lack of a
precision requirement than as a decision-quality metric. At $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$,
all three algorithms show zero bias, as expected from the symmetry of the fair coin
case. Once $\theta_{\rm true}$ deviates from $0.5$, HDI+ROPE's bias grows markedly: its
median bias approaches ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$, with the upper
IQR extending substantially further---because HDI+ROPE can stop on skewed samples before
sufficient data have accumulated to constrain the posterior.

The precision-based methods exhibit substantially smaller $\hat{\theta}$ bias, well
within the ROPE width across all $\theta_{\rm true}$ values, as a natural consequence
of their precision requirement. Among conclusive experiments within the ROPE, PitG
shows the higher bias than DPitG.\footnote{For inconclusive experiments (not shown),
where PitG contributes far more experiments than DPitG or HDI+ROPE, PitG's
$\hat{\theta}$ bias is slightly \textit{lower} than DPitG's. Considering
both conclusive and inconclusive experiments, both yield near-zero bias at all $\theta_{\rm true}$ values.}
Accounting for both conclusive and inconclusive
experiments, DPitG decision accuracy exceeds PitG's at every $\theta_{\rm true}$ value
(cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}, right panel).

DPitG is decisively superior to PitG near and within the ROPE, at the cost of a larger
median $N_{\rm stop}$.
Figure~\ref{fig:stop_iterations_by_truth_conclusive} quantifies this cost for all three
algorithms, restricting to conclusive outcomes since inconclusive experiments always stop
at $N_{\rm max}$ and their stop iteration therefore carries no information about
algorithm behaviour; including HDI+ROPE also illustrates why the precision
requirement matters.

HDI+ROPE shows distinctly different behaviour inside and outside the ROPE.
Within the ROPE, $N_{\rm stop}$ loosely tracks $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$, but
with a wide IQR reflecting the absence of a precision criterion. Outside the ROPE,
$N_{\rm stop}$ falls well below $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$: HDI+ROPE correctly
rejects $\theta_{\rm null}$ much faster than precision-based methods, but does so on
the basis of imprecise posteriors, producing the strong $\hat{\theta}$ bias
(cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:success_by_truth_conclusive}).

PitG stops tightly around $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$ by design, making it the
natural reference for understanding the precision cost. The contrast with the planned
quantity $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ reveals an important asymmetry: because
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta)$ is maximised at $\theta=0.5$ and decreases as $\theta$ moves
towards $0$ or $1$, the direction of the gap depends on where $\theta_{\rm true}$ lies
relative to $\theta_{\rm null}$.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$, any deviation of $\theta_{\rm true}$ from $0.5$ implies
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true}) < N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$: the expected stop
iteration of $N_{\rm goal}=599$ is a conservative upper bound on $N_{\rm stop}$,
and experiments stop earlier whenever the truth differs from the null.

For $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$, the picture is asymmetric. When $\theta_{\rm true}>
\theta_{\rm null}$ (further from $0.5$), $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})<
N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ and experiments stop sooner than planned. When
$\theta_{\rm true}<\theta_{\rm null}$ (closer to $0.5$), however,
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})>N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null}=0.7)\approx 503$:
the planned budget underestimates the samples required, and
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ acts as a lower bound rather than an upper one.

In general, $N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ serves as the researcher's advance
planning estimate: a conservative upper bound when $\theta_{\rm null}$ lies at the
precision maximum (e.g.\ $\theta_{\rm null}=0.5$), and a floor otherwise.
The researcher should set $N_{\rm max}$ according to their resources, with
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm null})$ as the baseline (here $N_{\rm max}=1{,}500$).

DPitG's $N_{\rm stop}$ equals or exceeds PitG's within the ROPE and in the vicinity
of its boundaries, the extra iterations being precisely what accounts for the higher
conclusiveness seen in Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}. Once $\theta_{\rm true}$
is far enough from the ROPE, DPitG converges to PitG and both stop at
$N_{\rm goal}(\theta_{\rm true})$.

  \textit{Solid line}: conclusiveness ratio
  (cf.\ Figure~\ref{fig:conclusiveness_rates}).
  \textit{Dot-dashed line}: median $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio.
  \textit{Band}: interquartile range of the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio.
  \textit{Vertical dashed line}: ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}=0.55$.}
  \label{fig:stop_conclusiveness_ratios}
\end{figure}

The conclusiveness ratio substantially exceeds the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio throughout,
particularly within and immediately outside the ROPE. Strikingly, the conclusiveness
ratio remains above 1 even where the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio approaches 1---that is, where
both algorithms stop at the same iteration---which occurs for roughly
$0.60 \lesssim \theta_{\rm true} \lesssim 0.63$: DPitG gains conclusive outcomes at minor
extra sampling cost in this region. The lower IQR band confirms that a substantial
fraction of DPitG experiments stop exactly when PitG does, keeping the median
$N_{\rm stop}$ ratio low. At $\theta_{\rm true}=0.5$, the conclusiveness ratio is
$2.66$ (cf.\ Table~\ref{tab:goal_impact} for how this ratio varies with $\omega_{\rm goal}$)
while the $N_{\rm stop}$ ratio has an IQR of $1.00$--$1.29$ (median $1.04$,
mean $1.21$): roughly a $2.7\times$ gain in conclusiveness at a median cost of only
$4\%$ more samples.

The apparent flattening of both ratios near ${\rm ROPE}_{\rm max}$ is an artefact of
the finite cap $N_{\rm max}=1{,}500$; with a larger cap both curves would peak sharply
at the boundary.
The DPitG advantage documented here generalises: qualitatively similar conclusiveness
gains hold for $\theta_{\rm null}=0.7$ (as shown in the right panel of
Figure~\ref{fig:stop_iterations_by_truth_conclusive}) and across $\omega_{\rm goal}$
values (see Section~\ref{sec:goal_impact} for the fair coin case).
